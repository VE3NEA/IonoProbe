SSN and SFI

Long distance HF propagation is possible due to the ionosphere, a set of conductive layers in the upper atmosphere that refract radio waves back to the Earth (and also absorb them). To estimate the level of refraction and absorption, it is important to know the degree of ionization in the ionosphere (the number of electrons per cubic meter). Strong ionization results in the refraction of higher frequencies but also increases the signal absorption.

The main source of electrons in the ionosphere is the ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun. Unfortunately, real time UV flux data are not available on the Web. Two surrogate parameters are used to estimate the level of ionization, Sunspot Number (SSN) and Solar Flux Index (SFI).


Sunspot Number: the daily number of sunspots and sunspot groups obtained from the optical observations.


Solar Flux Index: the intensity of solar radio emission with a wavelength of 10.7 cm (about 2800 MHz).


Both parameters are statistically correlated with the level of atmospheric ionization, but there is no direct relationship: daily variations of SSN or SFI are not always associated with changes in the ionosphere. These parameters are useful as trend indicators: if SSN and SFI have been high for a number of days, you can expect higher values of MUF and stronger absoprtion.
