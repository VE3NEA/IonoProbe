Kp and Ap

While the UV radiation creates free electrons in the ionosphere, the magnetic field of the Earth keeps those electrons in place. Being charged particles, electrons spiral around the magnetic field lines and stay in the layer where they were created, instead of flying away. The geofield serves as a vessel for the electrons, allowing the electron density in the F layer reach the levels sufficient for ionospheric HF propagation.

This mechanism works fine only when the geomagnetic field is quiet. During the geostorms the vessel cracks, and electrons leak from the F layer. The MUF drops considerably, and the upper bands close. Geomagnetic storms last from several hours to a few days, then it may take solar UV a few more days to restore the original electron density in the F layer.

Two space weather parameters indicate the state of the geomagnetic field, the Kp index and the Ap index.


Kp index: a 3-hourly planetary index of geomagnetic activity expressed in the logarithmic units on a scale from 0 to 9.


Ap index: a daily planetary index of geomagnetic activity derived from the 8    3-hourly values of Kp. Unlike Kp, Ap is expressed in linear units on a scale from 0 to about 400.

Both indices are equally important: Kp tells you if a geostorm is in progress, and Ap indicates whether the storm has just started or it has been developing for a while.

With IonoProbe, you are not limited to the current values of the indices. The history of Kp for the last few days provides detailed information about the development of the storm and allows you to estimate its consequencies more accurately.
